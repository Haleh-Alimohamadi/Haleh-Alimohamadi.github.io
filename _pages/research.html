Mechanics of Biological Membranes

Biological phenomena in small length scales of nanometer are always fascinating. One of the most compelling natural puzzles is the ability of cells to alter and adjust their shape so rapidly depending on the biophysical and biochemical conditions. Cells are the basic building blocks of living organisms. One key element of a cell's viability is the existence of a self-assembled lipid bilayer known as the plasma membrane. From a mechanical point of view, plasma membrane is a continuous elastic shell that bends and deforms in response to external stresses. To understand how molecular machineries can generate forces and deform cell membranes, I am developing theoretical frameworks that can tightly integrate experimental observations with computational models.

  • The Biconcave Morphology of Red Blood Cells
Human red blood cells have a unique donut (biconcave disk) shape. Deviations from this biconcavity interfere with RBC function in fatal diseases such as anemias, sickle cell disease, and malaria. Building upon our mathematical framework for membrane mechanics, we proposed a new molecular mechanism that could be responsible for maintaining the unique biconcave shape of the RBC. We showed that a non-uniform force distribution coupled with membrane tension characterized the shape of a RBC. Experimental measurements of myosin motor proteins density (performed by our collaborator professor Velia Fowler) in the dimple and rim regions of RBCs has validated our prediction of non-uniform force distribution in a biconcave RBC [Read more].
  • The Beading Structure of Membrane Nanotubes
Membrane nanotubes are intracellular structures that can connect cells over long distances, providing a highway for the transfer of molecules between the cells. Nanotubes are typically thin cylindrical membranes and their shapes play an important role in regulating their functionality. For example, in disease states such as cancer, the change in the morphology of the nanotubes to bead-like structures can lead to the failure of their functionality. Currently, the mechanism underlying the nanotube shape transformation is a matter of debate. Based on our mathematical framework, we suggested that the heterogeneity in the membrane properties and the local phase separation of protein are the key parameters for regulating the shape of nanotubes [Read more].

Non-newtonian Blood Flow in Stenosed Arteries

Blood Flow in Stenosed Arteries Non-newtonian Blood Flow in Stenosed Arteries Atherosclerosis is a fatal disease in which plaques build up inside our blood vessels. The plaques may grow with time to such an extent that they can significantly obstruct our arteries causing heart attack and stroke. Every year, over 10 million people are losing their lives because of a heart attack all over the world. In the last few decades, the use of external magnetic fields has become popular as a safe treatment to control the kinematics and the dynamics of blood flow particularly in the stenosed arteries. In order to understand how the imposed magnetic field can alter the behavior of blood flow near the atherosclerotic plaques, we developed a general code using the finite volume method (FVM) based on the pseudo-transient SIMPLE algorithm. Based on our results, a bi-directional magnetic field can suppress vortex formation and lower the wall shear stress distributions in the rear side of the plaque which can remarkably decrease the chance of heart attack.
